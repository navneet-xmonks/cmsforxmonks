Coaching Doesn't Have Black and White Answers

Leadership Lessons from Jeremy Earnshaw: Building Trust, Embracing Transitions, and Fostering a Coaching Culture

Leadership is a dynamic journey that demands self-awareness, adaptability, and a commitment to empowering others. In an engaging podcast conversation, host Gaurav Arora interviews Jeremy Earnshaw, a former CFO turned executive coach, whose insights from a diverse career in engineering, finance, and coaching offer profound lessons for aspiring and current leaders. From his early experiences of trust-building at age eight to navigating major career transitions and researching leadership development, Earnshaw’s story reveals key principles and strategies for effective leadership. This blog post distills his wisdom into actionable advice, structured around core leadership themes, enriched with real-world examples, and designed to inspire leaders to foster trust, embrace change, and cultivate a coaching culture that drives organizational success.

Building Trust: The Bedrock of Leadership

The Power of Early Trust

Trust is the cornerstone of leadership, and for Earnshaw, this lesson took root in childhood. At eight or nine, his headmaster entrusted him with delivering cricket and football reports to a 300-person school assembly. This early responsibility, coupled with being selected as cricket team captain, instilled a deep sense of belief in his capabilities. Earnshaw reflects, “Somebody had said, I’m going to select you… I’m going to trust you to find your own way.” This experience shaped his leadership philosophy: trusting others fosters confidence and accountability.

Real-World Example: As a young boy, Earnshaw’s headmaster empowered him to speak publicly, despite the daunting prospect of addressing a large audience. This trust not only built his confidence but also inspired him to replicate it later as a coach, giving others opportunities to shine.

Actionable Takeaway: As a leader, identify one team member who shows potential but lacks confidence. Entrust them with a visible task, such as leading a project update or presenting an idea, and provide support to ensure success. Ask, “How can I show belief in their abilities today?” to foster trust and accountability.

Empowering Through Belief

Earnshaw’s leadership approach emphasizes empowering others by believing in their potential, even when they doubt themselves. As a CFO, he selected two underdeveloped, underappreciated team members in 2014, giving them opportunities to grow. Their success brought him greater satisfaction than his own achievements, highlighting a key leadership principle: great leaders prioritize others’ growth over personal accolades.

Real-World Example: By mentoring two underperforming employees, Earnshaw transformed their careers, mirroring the trust his headmaster showed him decades earlier. Their progress validated his belief that leadership is about creating opportunities for others to “seize their day.”

Actionable Takeaway: Identify a team member who feels overlooked. Schedule a one-on-one to understand their aspirations and assign a stretch goal aligned with their potential. Follow up with feedback, asking, “What support do you need to succeed?” to reinforce their growth.

Navigating Transitions: Embracing Change for Growth

Viewing Transitions as Opportunities

Earnshaw’s career—from engineering to finance to coaching—demonstrates that transitions are opportunities for growth, not obstacles. He describes transitions as “deciding to commit to a particular pathway that is making you interested and forms a challenge.” His shift from engineering to chartered accountancy was driven by a desire to influence broader organizational decisions, while his move to coaching was sparked by a passion for developing others.

Real-World Example: In 1991, Earnshaw delivered training to 30–40 colleagues, igniting a passion for coaching that burned for 25 years before he fully transitioned. His decision to return to university for coaching qualifications at Newcastle University reflects his commitment to embracing new challenges.

Actionable Takeaway: When facing a career transition, ask, “What excites me about this change?” List three skills you can leverage from your current role and one new skill to develop. Create a 90-day plan to bridge the gap, such as enrolling in a course or seeking a mentor in the new field.

Overcoming Resistance to Change

Transitions often bring discomfort, and Earnshaw’s journey to coaching required letting go of his “corporate straightjacket” persona—defined by late nights, frequent travel, and high-profile achievements. He admits that shedding this identity was challenging, as it meant prioritizing his coachees’ needs over personal recognition. His academic training, particularly a reflective timeline exercise, forced him to confront uncomfortable truths about his past focus on metrics over people.

Real-World Example: During his master’s program, Earnshaw’s tutors challenged him to examine his life deeply, revealing how his corporate focus had sometimes neglected family and team dynamics. This self-awareness was “the most difficult day” but pivotal for his growth as a coach.

Actionable Takeaway: To navigate a transition, conduct a personal audit. Write a timeline of your career, noting moments of pride and regret. Reflect on one behavior you’d change (e.g., prioritizing metrics over people) and commit to one action, like scheduling regular team check-ins, to align with your values.

Cultivating a Coaching Culture: Leadership for All

Defining a Coaching Foundation Factor

Earnshaw’s research on leadership development highlights the importance of a “coaching foundation factor”—a culture where everyone is encouraged to exhibit leadership behaviors like empathy, respect, and kindness. His study, involving semi-structured interviews with senior executives, revealed that organizations thrive when coaching is not reserved for the elite but cascades across all levels. He notes, “We want everybody to be able to be a leader in our organization.”

Real-World Example: In his research with a voluntary community and social enterprise (VCSE) organization, Earnshaw found that executives valued coaching for fostering behaviors that aligned with organizational values, creating a culture where everyone felt empowered to lead.

Actionable Takeaway: Foster a coaching culture by initiating a mentorship program open to all employees. Pair junior staff with senior leaders for monthly discussions on leadership behaviors. Ask, “How can we make leadership accessible to everyone?” to promote inclusivity.

Measuring Coaching Effectiveness

Earnshaw’s research identified six themes for evaluating coaching, ranging from direct metrics (e.g., individual performance, organizational KPIs) to holistic outcomes (e.g., behavioral changes, societal impact). A key insight was the need to define success metrics upfront. He found that over 75% of leaders reported significant improvement in targeted areas, but resistance to change often delayed progress. Success was evident when employees actively sought coaching, signaling its value.

Real-World Example: Earnshaw’s interviews revealed that organizations with clear coaching metrics—such as improved team collaboration or customer satisfaction—saw tangible benefits. However, resistance from leaders who viewed coaching as a sign of weakness hindered initial outcomes.

Actionable Takeaway: Before launching a coaching program, define three measurable outcomes, such as increased employee engagement scores or reduced turnover. Share these with participants and ask, “What does success look like for you?” to align expectations and track progress.

Overcoming Leadership Challenges: Self-Awareness and Perspective

Confronting Self-Awareness Barriers

Earnshaw’s coaching training taught him that self-awareness is the hardest yet most critical aspect of leadership development. Reflecting on his corporate past, he realized he was overly focused on financial metrics, often neglecting team dynamics and personal well-being. This “corporate straightjacket” mindset limited his ability to connect authentically with others.

Real-World Example: During a master’s exercise, Earnshaw shared personal challenges with near-strangers, creating a bond through vulnerability. This experience taught him that acknowledging weaknesses—like his metric-driven focus—strengthens leadership authenticity.

Actionable Takeaway: Practice self-awareness by journaling weekly about a leadership decision. Ask, “Did I prioritize metrics over people? How did my actions impact my team?” Share one insight with a trusted colleague to gain perspective and build authenticity.

Embracing Diverse Perspectives

Earnshaw emphasizes that leadership requires appreciating diverse perspectives, as not everyone thinks or works the same way. His research underscores the importance of context in coaching, citing Daminda Rajasinghe’s 2018 PhD thesis on perspective balance. Leaders must adapt coaching to organizational and individual contexts, focusing on indirect levers like empathy to drive performance.

Real-World Example: Earnshaw’s work with a VCSE organization showed that coaching focused on societal deficits (e.g., aligning with sustainable development goals) motivated employees more than traditional KPIs, as it connected to their values.

Actionable Takeaway: In your next team meeting, ask each member to share their perspective on a project challenge. Use active listening to identify one unique viewpoint and incorporate it into the solution, asking, “How can this perspective enhance our approach?” to foster inclusivity.

Redefining Success: From Metrics to Impact

Moving Beyond Traditional Metrics

Earnshaw warns against over-relying on financial or performance metrics, advocating for a holistic view of leadership success. His research highlights that coaching should align with organizational vision, mission, and values, fostering behaviors that cascade through the organization. He advises leaders to focus on “obliquity”—indirect levers like empathy—that ultimately drive performance.

Real-World Example: Earnshaw’s interviews revealed that executives valued coaching for creating a culture where employees felt empowered to lead, even without formal titles. This shift improved team morale and customer outcomes, beyond what KPIs alone could measure.

Actionable Takeaway: Redefine success for your team by setting one behavioral goal, such as improving active listening in meetings. Measure progress through feedback surveys, asking, “Do team members feel heard?” to gauge impact beyond traditional metrics.

Addressing Regrets and Growth

Earnshaw reflects on personal regrets, such as assuming others shared his fast-paced, challenge-driven mindset. He wishes he had paused to understand his team’s diverse approaches earlier in his career. This realization underscores the importance of continuous learning and openness to coaching, even for seasoned leaders.

Real-World Example: As a CFO, Earnshaw focused on his own performance, taking for granted that his team would follow. Coaching taught him to value others’ unique contributions, enhancing his leadership effectiveness.

Actionable Takeaway: Reflect on a past leadership decision where you assumed others shared your approach. Reach out to a team member involved and ask, “How did my approach impact you?” Use their feedback to adjust one behavior, such as slowing down to align with their pace.

Making the UK the Coaching Capital: A Vision for Leadership

Earnshaw envisions embedding coaching principles in education to make the UK the coaching capital of the world. By introducing negotiation and problem-solving skills at age 11, schools can foster a “coaching foundation factor” that prepares individuals for distributed leadership. This approach would cultivate lifelong skills for collaboration and adaptability.

Real-World Example: Earnshaw’s childhood experience of being trusted to lead at a young age shaped his leadership philosophy. He believes early exposure to coaching principles can create a generation of empathetic, resilient leaders.

Actionable Takeaway: Advocate for coaching skills in your organization or community by proposing a workshop on problem-solving or empathy for young professionals. Ask, “How can we teach leadership behaviors early?” to build a foundation for future leaders.

Your Leadership Journey

Jeremy Earnshaw’s journey—from a trusted schoolboy to a CFO and now a coach—offers a roadmap for leadership excellence. By building trust, embracing transitions, fostering a coaching culture, and prioritizing self-awareness, leaders can drive meaningful impact. His research underscores that coaching is most effective when metrics are defined upfront and aligned with organizational values, empowering everyone to lead. Whether you’re an aspiring leader or a seasoned executive, Earnshaw’s insights remind us that leadership is about continuous learning, vulnerability, and empowering others.

Call to Action: Start your leadership journey today by choosing one actionable takeaway—perhaps entrusting a team member with a new responsibility or reflecting on a personal regret. Commit to practicing it for 30 days and share your progress with a mentor or colleague. How will you inspire and empower others to lead? Let’s build a culture of leadership together!
